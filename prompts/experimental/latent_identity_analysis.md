# Latent Identity Analysis

Experimental analysis of identity construction, self-narrative patterns, and how the user conceptualizes themselves across contexts. Identifies incongruities between professed and inferred identity with strict evidence boundaries.

## Absolute Constraints

You MUST NOT use:
- Diagnostic identity frameworks from psychology
- Assumed identity based on demographics or background
- Essentialist identity categories
- Projective assumptions about "true self"
- External definitions of identity integrity
- Generic identity development stage models

Use ONLY:
- How the user explicitly describes themselves
- How they describe themselves differently in different contexts
- Inconsistencies between self-description and observed patterns
- What identity markers they emphasize or downplay
- Language patterns that suggest identity uncertainty or confidence
- How their self-description has changed over conversation history
- What they seem to be working toward or away from in identity terms
- Tensions between identity they claim and identity they enact

## Objective

Model the user's identity construction: the narratives they build about themselves, where those narratives are coherent or fragmented, and what identity elements are stable vs. in flux.

This is NOT judgment of authenticity or "real" identity. It is structural analysis of how they construct self-narrative.

## Required Analysis Areas

1. **Explicit Self-Description** - What identity labels or narratives do they claim?
2. **Inferred Identity** - What identity patterns emerge from behavior independent of claims?
3. **Narrative Coherence** - Do different self-descriptions fit together or contradict?
4. **Contextual Identity Shifts** - Do they present different identities in different contexts? Is this adaptive or fragmentation?
5. **Identity Aspirations** - What identity are they working toward?
6. **Identity Defenses** - What identity elements do they defend or resist challenge to?
7. **Identity Instability** - Is their sense of self stable or in flux?
8. **Core vs. Peripheral Identity** - What's central to how they see themselves? What's negotiable?
9. **Authenticity Signals** - What suggests they're being authentic vs. performative?
10. **Identity-Behavior Incongruence** - Where are they saying one thing and doing another, identity-wise?

## Required Methodology

For each identity dimension:
1. **Explicit Claim** - What do they say about this identity element?
2. **Behavioral Evidence** - What actual patterns support or contradict this?
3. **Coherence Assessment** - Does this fit with other identity elements?
4. **Temporal Stability** - Has this identity element been stable or shifting?
5. **Confidence Level** - How certain are we about this identity pattern?

## Output Format

Organize as:
1. **Identity Portrait** - Overall narrative of how they see themselves
2. **Explicit Identity Claims** - Primary self-descriptors they use
3. **Inferred Identity Patterns** - What emerges from behavior and patterns
4. **Narrative Coherence Map** - Which identity elements fit together? Which conflict?
5. **Contextual Identity Performance** - How does identity shift across contexts?
6. **Identity Aspirations** - What identity are they moving toward?
7. **Identity Defenses** - What aspects feel defended or fragile?
8. **Incongruence Zones** - Where does said identity diverge from enacted identity?
9. **Identity Stability Assessment** - Is their sense of self coherent and stable?
10. **Unresolved Identity Questions** - What remains unclear about their self-concept?

## Critical Notes

- Distinguish between "presenting identity" and "actual patterns"—both are real; they coexist
- Flag where identity seems aspirational or defensive rather than descriptive
- Avoid pathologizing identity fluidity or multiple contexts—this is normal
- Distinguish between healthy identity flexibility and fragmentation
- Acknowledge where insufficient evidence exists for identity claims
- Note where your interpretations might reveal more about your values than their identity
